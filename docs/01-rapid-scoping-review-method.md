# Rapid Scoping Review Method

## Review Type

This is a **rapid scoping review** for course ideation and SLP proposal development. It is not a full systematic review. The aim is to map the strongest and most relevant evidence quickly enough to support five course concepts, module themes, learning outcomes and assessment ideas.

## Review Questions

1. What AI-related HRM problems are managers and practitioners currently facing?
2. Which AI + HRM topics are mature enough to become short learning programmes?
3. What risks, governance issues and capability gaps should be built into each course?
4. How can each SLP combine academic credibility with workplace application?

## Scope

- **Period prioritised:** 2019-2026, with older foundational HRM sources included where still influential.
- **Disciplines:** HRM, management, labour studies, responsible AI, information systems, people analytics, sustainability and work design.
- **Source types:** peer-reviewed articles, official standards, public policy/regulatory sources, labour-market reports and selected working papers where the topic is too new for settled journal literature.
- **Geographic framing:** South Africa and African distance-learning relevance, with global labour-market and AI governance evidence used where directly applicable.

## Search Strategy

Searches used combinations of the following terms:

- "AI" OR "artificial intelligence" OR "generative AI" OR "LLM" OR "ChatGPT" OR "Claude"
- "human resource management" OR "HRM" OR "people analytics" OR "talent management" OR "algorithmic hiring"
- "work redesign" OR "job redesign" OR "future of work" OR "skills"
- "AI governance" OR "responsible AI" OR "risk management" OR "AI management system"
- "agentic AI" OR "AI agents" OR "workflow automation" OR "human-in-the-loop"
- "green HRM" OR "sustainable HRM" OR "Green AI" OR "sustainable AI"

Source pools included Google/web search, publisher pages, official standards and policy pages, UNISA public SLP pages, ILO, WEF, NIST, ISO, OECD, UNESCO, South African POPIA resources, and peer-reviewed article pages where accessible.

## Inclusion Logic

Included sources had to meet at least one of these criteria:

- Directly address AI in HRM, people analytics, hiring, workforce planning, employee management or work redesign.
- Provide authoritative governance or legal guidance relevant to HR AI adoption.
- Offer credible labour-market evidence on AI, skills and task transformation.
- Support one of the five SLP concepts with practical curriculum implications.

## Exclusion Logic

Sources were not prioritised if they were:

- Purely promotional vendor material without reusable educational value.
- Only about technical AI model engineering with no HRM, work or management link.
- Opinion pieces with no evidence, framework or implementation insight.
- Duplicative of stronger official or peer-reviewed sources.

## Limitations

- The search is rapid and targeted, not exhaustive.
- Some AI-agent literature is emerging through working papers and industry practice, so the course should present this area as developing rather than settled.
- HRM laws and AI regulations are changing quickly. Final course materials must be updated before launch.
- South African AI-specific regulation is still developing, so POPIA and general labour/constitutional principles should be paired with international AI governance frameworks.

## Citation Approach

Course concept documents use concise in-text source mentions plus reference lists with URLs. During full course development, the academic team should convert references into the citation style required by the host college or programme approval process.
