# Learner Study Guide

## Welcome

Welcome to **Agentic HRM and Workflow Automation for Managers**. This programme is for learners who need to understand how AI agents and workflow automation may reshape HR operations, service delivery and people-management processes.

The course is not about building AI agents from code. It is about deciding where agents may be useful, where they may be risky, and how to design workflows where humans remain accountable.

## How to Use This Study Guide

Each week you should:

1. Read the weekly study notes.
2. Complete the workflow or governance activity.
3. Add evidence to your final workflow blueprint.
4. Record risks, assumptions and controls.
5. Participate in discussion or peer review.

## Core Principle

Do not automate a broken process. Agentic AI should be considered only after the workflow, data, permissions, risks and human review points are understood.

## Study Unit 1: From Chatbots to AI Agents in HRM

### Key Ideas

A chatbot mainly responds to user queries. A copilot assists a person with a task. Workflow automation follows predefined rules. An AI agent may plan steps, call tools, retrieve information, draft outputs and trigger actions across systems.

In HRM, possible agentic use cases include employee query triage, onboarding support, recruitment coordination, learning administration, policy retrieval, HR case routing and workflow reminders. These can improve service speed, but they also create risks if the agent has too much access, makes unsupported decisions or acts without proper human approval.

### Practical Focus

You will classify AI use cases by autonomy level and identify which tasks should be assisted, automated or kept human-led.

### Reflection Question

Which HRM or workplace task would become risky if an AI agent could act without approval?

## Study Unit 2: HR Workflow Mapping and Automation Readiness

### Key Ideas

Agentic AI should start with workflow mapping. A workflow map shows steps, people, systems, data, handoffs, decisions and exceptions. It helps identify where automation may help and where risk is too high.

Automation readiness depends on:

- Process clarity.
- Repetition and volume.
- Data quality.
- Standardisation.
- Risk level.
- System integration.
- Human review capacity.
- Governance maturity.
- User trust.

### Practical Focus

You will select one HRM or people-management workflow and assess whether it is ready for agentic automation.

### Reflection Question

Is the selected workflow clear enough to automate, or does it first need simplification?

## Study Unit 3: Human-in-the-Loop Agent Workflow Design

### Key Ideas

Human-in-the-loop design means humans are assigned specific review, approval and escalation responsibilities. It is not enough to say "a human will check". A good design explains who checks, what they check, when they check, what authority they have and what evidence is recorded.

Agentic HRM workflows need:

- Defined agent role.
- Permission limits.
- Data-access rules.
- Approval gates.
- Escalation triggers.
- Logs and audit trails.
- Prohibited actions.
- Incident response.

### Practical Focus

You will create a permission and control map for an agent-assisted HRM workflow.

### Reflection Question

What should this agent never be allowed to do?

## Study Unit 4: Pilot, Evaluation, Governance and Scaling

### Key Ideas

Because agentic AI is still emerging, organisations should start with small, low-risk pilots. A good pilot has a narrow scope, clear controls, measurable outcomes, defined users and a stopping rule.

Pilot success should not be measured only by speed. It should also consider quality, risk incidents, employee experience, manager trust, fairness, cost and whether human workload changed in a healthy way.

### Practical Focus

You will design a low-risk pilot and decide what would justify scaling, revising, pausing or retiring it.

### Reflection Question

What evidence would convince you that the workflow is ready to scale?

## Final Portfolio

Your final portfolio is an **Agentic HRM Workflow Blueprint**. It should help a manager, HR leader or governance committee decide whether an agent-assisted workflow should proceed to pilot.
