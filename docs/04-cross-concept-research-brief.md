# Cross-Concept Research Brief

## Executive Synthesis

The five proposed SLPs respond to a shared shift in work: AI is no longer only a technical tool used by specialists. It is entering managerial routines, HR service delivery, talent decisions, employee communication, learning design, analytics and workflow automation. The evidence base suggests that the strongest educational opportunity is not to teach managers to "use AI" in a generic way, but to teach them to redesign work, govern risk and make better people decisions.

## Theme 1: AI in HRM Is a Work Redesign Problem

Generative AI can assist with writing, summarising, classification, ideation and decision preparation. Field evidence such as Brynjolfsson, Li and Raymond's study on generative AI at work suggests productivity gains are possible, especially for less experienced workers in some task settings. However, those effects are not automatic and may vary by task, worker expertise and organisational context.

Curriculum implication:

- SLP 01 should centre on workflow redesign, not just prompt engineering.
- Learners should compare current and future workflows, identify human review points and define measures of quality.

## Theme 2: HRM AI Is High-Stakes Because It Affects Opportunity

AI in HRM can influence hiring, promotion, training access, performance management and employee monitoring. Tambe, Cappelli and Yakubovich identify persistent HR AI challenges: complex human phenomena, small datasets, accountability issues and employee reactions. Köchling and Wehner's systematic review and later algorithmic hiring literature reinforce that bias and discrimination are central concerns in HR AI.

Curriculum implication:

- SLP 02 should treat fairness, transparency and accountability as core managerial capabilities.
- POPIA, NIST AI RMF and ISO/IEC 42001 should be translated into practical HR governance artefacts.

## Theme 3: People Analytics Needs Evidence Discipline

People analytics can improve HR decision-making, but only when questions, measures and interpretations are valid. HR dashboards can mislead when managers confuse correlation with causation, ignore data quality or treat AI-generated analysis as authoritative. The course portfolio should frame analytics as structured decision support.

Curriculum implication:

- SLP 03 should teach learners to formulate decision questions before selecting metrics.
- AI-assisted analytics activities should include uncertainty notes, bias checks and ethical considerations.

## Theme 4: Agentic AI Is Emerging, Promising and Risky

AI agents introduce new possibilities because they can plan, use tools and act across workflows. The literature is newer and less settled than general HR analytics or responsible AI. Current evidence suggests a need to distinguish automation from augmentation and to preserve human agency. HRM is a sensitive domain for agentic workflows because agents may handle employee data, trigger communications or shape candidate and employee experiences.

Curriculum implication:

- SLP 04 should be explicitly positioned as a frontier course.
- Learners should design pilots, permission maps, escalation rules and audit trails before discussing scale.

## Theme 5: AI Adoption Should Be Linked to Sustainability and Green HRM

Green HRM research shows that HR practices can support environmental management through recruitment, training, performance, rewards and employee involvement. Green AI research adds that AI systems have environmental footprints and should be selected and governed responsibly. This creates a distinctive course niche: AI-enabled sustainable work transformation.

Curriculum implication:

- SLP 05 should connect Green HRM with digital work and AI adoption.
- Learners should design green skills plans, employee involvement mechanisms and sustainable AI use-case reviews.

## Evidence Gaps

- There is limited Africa-specific empirical research on generative AI in HRM practice.
- Agentic AI in HRM is still emerging, with more conceptual and prototype work than mature field evidence.
- Many HR AI studies focus on recruitment, leaving gaps in employee relations, HR development, public sector HRM and sustainability.
- Legal and governance requirements are evolving, so materials should be reviewed before each intake.

## Proposal Positioning

The strongest institutional positioning is a portfolio of applied, manager-facing SLPs that:

- Uses AI as a lens for HRM transformation.
- Keeps human judgment and responsible governance central.
- Serves both professional upskilling and postgraduate preparation.
- Produces workplace-ready artefacts as assessment outputs.
- Can be expanded later into a certificate pathway or stackable micro-credential sequence, subject to UNISA rules.
