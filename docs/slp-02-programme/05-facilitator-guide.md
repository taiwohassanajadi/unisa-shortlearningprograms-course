# Facilitator Guide

## Facilitator Role

The facilitator should help learners translate responsible AI principles into practical governance routines. The course should feel like a governance and assurance workshop, not an abstract ethics seminar.

## Teaching Principles

- Keep legal and technical concepts accessible.
- Connect every principle to an HRM or people-management decision.
- Emphasise evidence, controls and accountability.
- Avoid giving legal advice; teach governance awareness and escalation.
- Use fictional or anonymised cases.
- Encourage learners to document assumptions and uncertainty.

## Suggested Weekly Facilitation Notes

### Week 1

Start with familiar HR use cases. Ask learners where AI could already be entering employment decisions. Establish that the course focuses on governance before implementation.

### Week 2

Translate responsible AI principles into plain language. Ask learners to define what fairness, transparency and human oversight would look like in a real HR process.

### Week 3

Help learners identify affected persons. Push beyond the organisation's benefits and ask who may carry the risk if the system fails.

### Week 4

Introduce POPIA through practical HR data examples. Focus on purpose, minimality, information quality, openness, security safeguards and automated decision-making.

### Week 5

Compare frameworks. Learners do not need to memorise every clause. They should understand how NIST, ISO, OECD and EU AI Act reference points support governance questions.

### Week 6

Discuss vendor accountability. Many organisations buy AI-enabled HR tools without understanding data flows, model limits or audit rights. Use the vendor questionnaire worksheet.

### Week 7

Teach bias as a system issue, not only a model issue. Historical practices, job requirements, labels, proxies and managerial interpretation can all create unfair outcomes.

### Week 8

Focus on fairness review memos. Learners should make practical recommendations and not simply list risks.

### Week 9

Run an AI impact assessment workshop. Encourage learners to be specific about affected groups, severity, likelihood and controls.

### Week 10

Build the governance operating model. The key output is clarity: who owns, approves, reviews, monitors, escalates and audits.

### Week 11

Use an incident simulation. Ask what happens if the system rejects qualified candidates, exposes employee data or triggers a worker complaint.

### Week 12

Final presentations should defend a recommendation: approve, approve with controls, defer, or reject.

## Feedback Guidance

When giving feedback, focus on:

- Does the learner understand the use case?
- Are affected persons clearly identified?
- Are POPIA risks handled practically?
- Are fairness controls meaningful?
- Is human oversight concrete?
- Is the control matrix auditable?
- Is the final recommendation defensible?

## Common Learner Challenges

| Challenge | Facilitator Response |
|---|---|
| Learner writes broad AI ethics statements | Ask for concrete controls and evidence |
| Learner assumes vendor claims are enough | Require vendor documentation and audit questions |
| Learner ignores affected workers or candidates | Return to stakeholder and harm mapping |
| Learner treats human oversight vaguely | Ask who reviews, when, how and with what authority |
| Learner wants a yes/no legal answer | Reframe as risk assessment and escalation |

## Suggested Discussion Prompts

- What HR AI uses should require executive approval?
- When is AI assistance acceptable but automated decision-making unacceptable?
- What should a candidate or employee be told about AI use?
- What evidence should auditors ask for?
- When should an HR AI system be paused or withdrawn?
