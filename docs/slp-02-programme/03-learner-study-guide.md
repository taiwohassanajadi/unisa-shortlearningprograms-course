# Learner Study Guide

## Welcome

Welcome to **Responsible AI Governance in HRM: Ethics, Risk and Compliance**. This programme is for people who need to make, review or govern decisions about AI use in employment and people-management contexts.

The course is practical and non-technical. It does not teach learners to build AI models. It teaches learners to ask the right governance questions before AI is used in hiring, screening, performance management, learning, employee monitoring, workforce planning or HR service delivery.

## How to Use This Study Guide

Each week you should:

1. Read the weekly study notes.
2. Complete the governance activity.
3. Add evidence to your final governance pack.
4. Participate in the discussion or peer review activity.
5. Update your personal AI governance checklist.

## Core Principle

AI governance in HRM is about people, rights and accountability. A technically impressive system can still be inappropriate if it is unfair, opaque, intrusive, poorly monitored or unsupported by human review.

## Study Unit 1: Responsible AI Principles for HRM

### Key Ideas

Responsible AI principles include fairness, accountability, transparency, privacy, robustness, security, explainability, human oversight and contestability. In HRM these principles are not abstract. They affect who gets shortlisted, who receives training, who is monitored, who is promoted and who can challenge an unfair decision.

HRM is high-stakes because employment affects income, dignity, opportunity and wellbeing. Learners should therefore treat AI in HR as a governance issue, not simply a digital efficiency tool.

### Practical Focus

You will identify one HR AI or workplace AI use case and map stakeholders, affected persons and possible harms.

### Reflection Question

Who could be harmed if this AI system is inaccurate, biased, intrusive or misunderstood?

## Study Unit 2: Legal, Regulatory and Standards Context

### Key Ideas

POPIA governs the processing of personal information in South Africa. HR AI systems may process candidate information, employee records, performance data, biometric information, health-related information, disciplinary information or monitoring data. These uses require careful attention to accountability, lawful processing, minimality, purpose specification, information quality, openness, security safeguards and data subject participation.

International frameworks are useful because AI-specific governance is developing quickly. NIST AI RMF provides a risk management structure. ISO/IEC 42001 provides an organisational AI management system framing. OECD AI Principles give a human-centred policy foundation. The EU AI Act is useful as an international reference point because employment-related AI is treated as a high-risk area in many cases.

### Practical Focus

You will build a POPIA and responsible AI compliance checklist for an HR AI use case.

### Reflection Question

What evidence would show that your organisation considered privacy, purpose, fairness and human oversight before using the system?

## Study Unit 3: Bias, Discrimination and Fairness Auditing

### Key Ideas

Bias can enter HR AI systems through historical data, incomplete data, proxy variables, biased labels, poorly designed assessments, uneven access to technology or careless interpretation of outputs. Fairness auditing does not mean proving a system is perfect. It means asking structured questions, documenting evidence, monitoring outcomes and creating routes for review or appeal.

Learners should pay attention to both direct and indirect discrimination. A variable may appear neutral but still disadvantage a protected or vulnerable group.

### Practical Focus

You will write a fairness and bias review memo for a selected HR AI scenario.

### Reflection Question

What groups might be disadvantaged by this system, and how would the organisation know?

## Study Unit 4: AI Governance Operating Model for HRM

### Key Ideas

Responsible AI governance requires roles, controls and evidence. A good governance operating model explains:

- Who owns the HR AI use case.
- Who approves it.
- Who reviews data protection and legal risk.
- Who tests fairness and quality.
- Who monitors outcomes.
- Who handles complaints or appeals.
- What evidence must be retained.
- When the system must be paused, changed or retired.

The Department of Auditing perspective is especially relevant here because HR AI governance requires controls, documentation, monitoring and audit evidence.

### Practical Focus

You will create an AI impact assessment and control matrix for your selected HR AI use case.

### Reflection Question

If an employee or applicant challenged this AI use case, what evidence would the organisation be able to produce?

## Final Portfolio

Your final portfolio is a **Responsible AI Governance Pack for HRM**. It should be practical enough that a governance committee, HR leader, audit team or department head could use it to review a real or fictional HR AI use case before implementation.
