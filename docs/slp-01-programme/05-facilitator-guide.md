# Facilitator Guide

## Facilitator Role

The facilitator should help learners connect AI concepts to real workplace practice. The tone should be practical, critical and confidence-building. Learners should leave the course able to make better decisions about AI use, not merely use more tools.

## Teaching Principles

- Keep the course non-technical.
- Use workplace examples from departments, teams, projects and HR contexts.
- Emphasise judgment, confidentiality, fairness and accountability.
- Encourage experimentation with low-risk tasks.
- Discourage uploading personal, sensitive or confidential information into public AI tools.
- Treat AI output as a draft requiring human review.

## Suggested Weekly Facilitation Notes

### Week 1

Focus on learner orientation and baseline experience. Many learners may already be using AI informally. Normalise experimentation but introduce the idea that workplace AI use needs guardrails.

### Week 2

Demonstrate LLM limitations. Use a deliberately vague prompt, then compare it with a structured prompt. Show how plausible errors can appear in confident language.

### Week 3

Guide learners to think in tasks rather than jobs. The key teaching move is to separate "AI will replace my job" from "AI may support some tasks and change how work is organised."

### Week 4

Run a prompt clinic. Ask learners to improve prompts by adding context, audience, constraints and output format.

### Week 5

Use people-management scenarios carefully. Avoid sensitive real employee cases. Use fictional examples for onboarding, role clarity, coaching preparation and communication.

### Week 6

Focus on quality assurance. Learners should learn to ask: What could be false? What is missing? Who should review this? What source or policy should be checked?

### Week 7

Introduce workflow mapping. Encourage learners to keep maps simple. A table with steps, actors, decisions and risks is acceptable.

### Week 8

Teach human-in-the-loop design. Emphasise that human review is not a vague instruction. It should specify who reviews, what they check, when they approve and when they escalate.

### Week 9

Introduce POPIA and responsible AI in accessible language. Focus on personal information, confidentiality, purpose limitation, consent where relevant, security and automated decisions.

### Week 10

Help learners design small pilots. Push back on over-large transformation plans. A good pilot is narrow, observable and reversible.

### Week 11

Work with learners on measurement. Encourage balanced metrics: time saved, output quality, employee experience, error reduction, risk incidents and learning.

### Week 12

Use final presentations as peer learning. Ask learners to explain what they would do, what they would not do and what they still need to verify.

## Feedback Guidance

When giving feedback, focus on:

- Is the use case specific?
- Does the learner protect confidential information?
- Are human review points clear?
- Is the workflow realistic?
- Are risks named honestly?
- Are success measures practical?

## Common Learner Challenges

| Challenge | Facilitator Response |
|---|---|
| Learner focuses only on prompts | Redirect to workflow, governance and measurement |
| Learner selects a sensitive case | Ask them to anonymise, fictionalise or choose a lower-risk process |
| Learner trusts AI output too quickly | Require source checking, review criteria and human sign-off |
| Learner chooses too broad a process | Narrow to one team-level workflow |
| Learner is afraid of AI | Emphasise low-risk experimentation and human accountability |

## Suggested Discussion Prompts

- What workplace tasks should AI never perform alone?
- What is the difference between saving time and improving work?
- How should a team disclose AI use in internal documents?
- Who is accountable when AI-assisted communication harms trust?
- What would make employees comfortable or uncomfortable with AI use in their department?
